The Process

How astrophotography
actually works.

You can't just point a camera at a galaxy and take a photo. What you see in these images is the result of hundreds of individual exposures, combined in post-processing to pull signal out of noise. Here's how it works.

01

Star Tracking

The Earth rotates, which means stars move across the frame during a long exposure. Without tracking, you get star trails instead of pinpoint stars. The NexStar 5SE uses a motorised equatorial mount that rotates at exactly the same speed as the Earth — in the opposite direction — keeping the target locked in the frame for minutes at a time.

02

Light Frames

These are your actual exposures of the target — the galaxy, nebula, or planet you're capturing. For deep sky objects I shoot hundreds of these, typically 30–120 seconds each, over multiple sessions. Each frame captures a tiny amount of signal (the actual light from your target) buried in noise. Stacking them together amplifies the signal while averaging out the random noise.

03

Calibration Frames

These aren't photos of the sky — they're technical images used to correct systematic errors in your sensor and optical train. Each type removes a different kind of noise or artefact from your final stack.

Dark Frames

Taken with the lens cap on, same exposure length and temperature as your lights. Captures thermal noise your sensor produces — subtracted from each light frame.

Add exact details per image ↑

Flat Frames

Short exposures of a uniformly lit surface (usually a white screen or dawn sky). Captures vignetting and dust shadows in your optical path — corrects uneven illumination.

Add exact details per image ↑

Bias Frames

Zero-length exposures that capture the baseline readout noise of the sensor. Used in combination with darks and flats for the most accurate calibration.

Add exact details per image ↑

Dark Flats

Same exposure as flat frames but with the lens cap on. Calibrates the calibration — removes the bias signal embedded in the flat frames themselves.

04

Stacking & Post-Processing

All frames go through stacking software — typically Siril or DeepSkyStacker — which aligns them, applies calibrations, and combines them into a single master image. From there it's post-processing: stretching the histogram to bring out faint detail, colour calibration, noise reduction, and sharpening. The galaxy you see in the final image is usually invisible in any single raw frame.

Long Exposure · Byron Bay NSW

Star Trails

This is what happens when you deliberately don't track. Rather than fighting the Earth's rotation, you embrace it — shooting dozens to hundreds of individual frames and stacking them with a "lighten" blending mode so each star's path accumulates into a streak. The circular arc pattern reveals the Earth's rotation axis. The bright lights below are Byron Bay. Shot on the Nikon D750.
